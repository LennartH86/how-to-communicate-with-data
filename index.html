Data Visualization History
Charles Joseph Minard
Napoleon's March to Moscow, 1869
  • Considered by many the greatest statistical graphic ever drawn
  • Line ColorEncodes direction — beige for the advance, black for the retreat
  • Line ThicknessRepresents army strength — 422,000 soldiers entered Russia, only 10,000 returned
  • Line HeightShows temperature during the deadly winter retreat
  • Six variables in a single image: army size, direction, location, time, temperature, geography
Data Visualization History
Florence Nightingale
Mortality Causes in the Crimean War, 1858
  • Nightingale was a pioneering statistician as much as a nurse
  • Wedge AreaEncodes the number of deaths — the larger the wedge, the more deaths
  • Clock OrientationRepresents time — each wedge is one month, rotating like a clock
  • ColorDistinguishes cause of death — blue for disease, red for wounds, black for other
  • Showed preventable disease killed far more soldiers than combat — driving hospital reform
Data Visualization History
John Snow
Cholera Outbreak in Soho, London, 1854
  • Dot PlacementEvery death plotted at its exact street address — a deceptively simple idea
  • Spatial ClusteringDeaths grouped tightly around the Broad Street water pump
  • Pump RemovalTaking the handle off stopped the outbreak — early spatial epidemiology
  • Miasma RefutedProximity to the pump was the real variable, not bad air quality
  • Map as ProofThe chart didn't just describe the data — it solved a crisis
Data Visualization History
Hamburg Cholera Epidemic
Hamburg vs. Altona, 1892
  • The Epidemic1892 — over 8,600 people killed in Hamburg in just a few weeks
  • The BorderHamburg and Altona shared the same streets, divided only by a city border
  • HamburgDrew drinking water unfiltered directly from the polluted River Elbe
  • AltonaFiltered its water — deaths dropped sharply right at the city border
  • The ProofThe map made the invisible visible: same street, completely different outcomes

Visual Encoding — What Made These Charts Work

Minard
Napoleon's March, 1869
  • Line color — direction of march (advance vs. retreat)
  • Line thickness — army strength (soldiers remaining)
  • Line height — temperature during the winter retreat
  • Geography — spatial context (rivers, cities, borders)
Nightingale
Crimean War Deaths, 1858
  • Wedge area — number of deaths (larger = more)
  • Clock orientation — time (each wedge = one month)
  • Color — cause of death (disease, wounds, other)
  • Radial layout — easy comparison across all months
John Snow
London Cholera, 1854
  • Dots on a map — each dot = one death, at its exact location
  • Spatial clustering — proximity to the Broad Street pump
  • Reference points — pump locations as anchors
  • Absence — the brewery and workhouse had safe water
Hamburg
Cholera Boundary, 1892
  • Dot density — concentrated in Hamburg, sparse in Altona
  • City border — makes the policy difference visible
  • Absence — empty space in Altona is as powerful as filled
  • Juxtaposition — same street, completely different outcomes

Pre-attentive Attributes

Our brains detect outliers, minima,
and maxima in under 250 milliseconds

Before we consciously "read" a chart, our visual system has already identified the biggest bar, the red dot, and the lone outlier.

This is called pre-attentive processing — and great data visualizations are designed to exploit it deliberately.

reliable AI

What We Learned Today

🎨
The Power of Visual Encoding
Minard, Nightingale, and Snow showed us that the right visual encoding turns data into discovery. Color, size, position, and area all carry meaning.
📐
Always Visualize Your Data
Anscombe's Quartet and the Datasaurus Dozen prove it: identical statistics can hide completely different shapes. Charts reveal what numbers hide.
⚡
Pre-attentive Attributes
Your audience processes color, size, and position before they consciously read a single number. Design with this in mind — highlight what matters.
🔁
The Analytics Workflow
Good analysis is iterative. Question → Data → Insight → Act → New Question. The squiggle becomes a clear line only through repeated attempts.
🤝
Trust is Everything
The recipient of your insight must trust you to trust the outcome. Transparency in your process — showing the work — is what builds that trust.
🚀
AI + Visual Analytics
Visual storytelling for analysts plus natural-language AI for everyone else. Together, they bring the power of data to 100% of the workforce.
